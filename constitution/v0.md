# Joka Source Foundation — Constitution v0

> **Status:** DRAFT v0 — complete for founder review and signature. Not yet ratified.
> This file is the authoritative governing document of JSF once ratified by the founding cell.

## Preamble

The Joka Source Foundation (JSF) exists to build software for the IIM Calcutta community that outlives the people who build it. IIM Calcutta's student body turns over completely every two years; without a structural mechanism for succession, every good project a batch builds dies with that batch's graduation. JSF's founding premise is that this is a solvable problem, not an inevitability — provided the institution, not any individual, holds the code, the governance, and the institutional memory.

JSF is rooted in IIM Calcutta (the Joka campus) and open-source-first by default: the code, the decisions, and the reasoning behind them are public unless a specific, logged exception applies. It is modelled on the CRISPR cell lifecycle first proven at IIIT Nagpur — small, autonomous project cells that spawn, operate, and either persist or retire gracefully, with knowledge carried forward at every transition (see `constitution/CRISPR-model-notes.md` for the full model and its public analogues: Apache's meritocracy and Attic, Haier's Rendanheyi microenterprises, Spotify's guilds, and sociocratic circle governance).

This constitution is founded by **Krishna Chaudhari** (`@truekrishna`) and **Satyam Anand** (`@chrometraum`), both members of the IIM Calcutta PGP 2025–2027 cohort, on 1 July 2026. It governs every project cell operating under the JSF umbrella, beginning with Joka CV, AlumERP, and Joka Connect.

## Mission & Founding Principles

**Mission.** Build software for IIM Calcutta that outlives its founders, and hand each project to the next cohort in better shape than it was received.

**Founding principles:**

1. **Continuity over authorship.** A project's value is measured by whether it survives the graduation of the people who started it, not by who gets credit for starting it. Every governance choice in this document favors continuity when continuity and founder convenience conflict.

2. **Openness by default.** Code, decisions, and their rationale are public. Exceptions exist only where privacy or legal exposure requires it (the Joka Connect PII policy is the clearest example), and every exception is named explicitly rather than assumed.

3. **Merit, not tenure, earns authority.** Governance rights are earned through demonstrated contribution — merged work, sustained engagement, cell-lead nomination — not through founding status, seniority, or informal proximity to the founders. This is what lets a foundation outlive its founders: the mechanism for replacing them is built in from day one.

4. **Cells are autonomous, not departments.** Each project cell (Joka CV, AlumERP, Joka Connect, and any that follow) owns its own roadmap, releases, and internal decisions. No cell needs another cell's permission to ship. Every cell needs the founding cell's approval only to exist, to change scope materially, or to retire.

5. **Retirement is a feature, not a failure.** A project that no longer serves its purpose should be archived cleanly, not kept alive out of sentiment or left to rot unmaintained. An explicit, falsifiable retirement condition is a required part of every cell's charter, not an afterthought.

6. **Service to the campus community.** JSF software exists to serve IIM Calcutta students, alumni, and staff. Where a decision trades off founder interest, cell convenience, and community benefit, community benefit wins.

## Organizational Form

JSF adopts the CRISPR cell lifecycle (spawn → operate → retire) as its organizing model, adapted from the framework built and proven at IIIT Nagpur (see `constitution/CRISPR-model-notes.md`).

**Founding Cell (Cell 0).** Krishna Chaudhari and Satyam Anand constitute the Founding Cell. Cell 0 holds JSF's constitutional DNA: this document, the default license, the trademark, and final authority over spawning and retiring project cells. Cell 0 cannot be dissolved by any single project cell, and no project cell decision can override this constitution.

**Project Cells.** A project cell is a self-managing unit with its own cell lead, backlog, and release authority, operating within the bounds this constitution sets. JSF launches with three project cells:

- **Joka CV** — a structured, canonical CV and hiring tool for IIM Calcutta alumni and recruiters.
- **AlumERP** — the parent alumni management platform (records, events, fundraising, career services), which other JSF projects may integrate with over time.
- **Joka Connect** — the alumni networking platform (successor to "Joka Jodi"), covering professional networking and alumni matchmaking, planned to integrate with AlumERP as its social layer. Because it carries personal data, Joka Connect operates under an additional PII policy (`projects/joka-connect/PII-POLICY.md`) that sits alongside, and does not weaken, this constitution.

Each cell's charter (`projects/{slug}/CHARTER.md`) states its scope, its responsible founder at spawn time, and — critically — an explicit, falsifiable retirement condition set at spawn, not invented after the fact.

**Alumni Intake Cell (function, not a standing body).** Contribution is the on-ramp into JSF governance. Any IIM Calcutta student or alum may contribute as soon as they open a pull request or a meaningful issue — no application or approval gate exists at this tier. Sustained, merged contribution earns commit rights within a cell's scope; sustained committer activity, plus cell-lead nomination, earns a governance seat. This ladder is described fully under Membership & Succession below and is what lets graduating cohorts hand off a cell rather than orphan it.

**Cross-cell guilds.** Functional expertise (design, backend, data) is expected to travel across cells informally as guilds — for example a Design Guild spanning Joka CV and AlumERP — so a contributor rotating off one cell's active roster still carries standards into the next. Guilds are not formal governance bodies in v0; formalizing them is listed under Open Questions.

**Retirement primitive.** A cell retires when its charter's stated retirement condition is met and the Founding Cell concurs, or by Founding Cell unanimous vote for cause (see Decision Rights and `governance/voting-rules.md`). Retirement moves `projects/{slug}/` to `archive/{slug}-{year}/`, requires a filed `RETROSPECTIVE.md`, and permanently burns the slug — it is never reused for a different project. This is the mechanism that makes "outliving us" concrete: a dead cell doesn't silently rot in the active tree, and its lessons don't disappear with it.

## Decision Rights

| Decision | Who Decides | Quorum |
|---|---|---|
| Spawn a new project cell | Founding Cell | Unanimous |
| Retire a project cell | Founding Cell (+ cell committers where charter condition is met) | Unanimous (Founding Cell) |
| Amend this constitution | Founding Cell | Unanimous during the founding period |
| Accept a new founding (Cell 0) member | Founding Cell | Unanimous |
| Sign a legal document on JSF's behalf | Founding Cell | Unanimous |
| Choose or change a project's license | Founding Cell | Unanimous |
| Choose a vendor / infrastructure provider | Responsible founder for the affected cell | 1 (informational to Founding Cell) |
| Manage JSF funds (grants, sponsorships) | Founding Cell | Unanimous |
| Day-to-day PR merge within a cell | Cell committers | 1 approval |
| Advisor appointment | Founding Cell | Majority |

This table mirrors, and does not override, `governance/voting-rules.md`, which remains the canonical process document (proposal → 14-day comment window → recorded vote → `governance/DECISIONS.md` entry). During the founding period — defined as lasting until the trigger named in `governance/voting-rules.md` is set and met — every Founding Cell decision requires both founders to agree, and either founder holds an effective veto until that period ends.

## Membership & Succession

**Becoming a member.** There is no application to become a JSF contributor. Opening a pull request or filing a substantive issue against any JSF repository makes you a contributor. Every commit requires DCO sign-off (`Signed-off-by:` per [developercertificate.org](https://developercertificate.org)); no separate CLA is required unless a specific sub-project onboards contributors outside the IIM Calcutta community, in which case the Founding Cell flags it and a legal advisor reviews before activation (`governance/LICENSING.md`).

**Advancement.** Contributor → Committer → Cell Governance Member is a contribution-gated ladder, not a tenure-gated one:

- **Contributor** — any merged PR or accepted issue.
- **Committer** — sustained merged contribution to a specific cell, earning merge rights within that cell's scope. The exact numeric threshold (merged-PR count and/or active-months) is left to each cell's charter rather than fixed foundation-wide here — see Open Questions.
- **Cell Governance Member** — sustained committer activity plus nomination by the sitting cell lead; eligible to participate in that cell's spawn/retirement/lead-succession decisions.
- **Advisor** — a non-voting, expertise-contributing role for people outside the contribution ladder, listed in `roster/advisors.yml`.

**Appointing a project lead.** A new cell's charter names a `responsible_founder` at spawn. As a cell matures, cell governance members may nominate, and the Founding Cell may ratify, a non-founder cell lead — the explicit mechanism by which a project cell survives its founding cohort's graduation. The nomination-and-ratification process itself is left to per-cell charters in v0; standardizing it foundation-wide is an Open Question.

**Replacing a project lead.** A cell lead may step down voluntarily (recorded in `governance/DECISIONS.md`) or be replaced by Founding Cell decision for cause (inactivity beyond a period defined in the cell's charter, or conduct inconsistent with `CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md`). Replacement does not require retiring the cell.

**Retiring a project without killing JSF.** Retirement is scoped to the cell, never to the foundation. When a cell's charter-stated retirement condition is met, or the Founding Cell votes unanimously for cause, the cell moves through the retirement primitive described above (archive, retrospective, slug burn) while JSF itself, its constitution, its other cells, and its roster continue unaffected. JSF's own dissolution — as distinct from a single cell's retirement — is not addressed in v0 and is listed under Open Questions.

**Alumni rotation.** Graduation is the expected, routine way contributors leave active cell work — it is not treated as an emergency. A cell's health check, prior to any retirement vote, includes whether it has active contributors independent of the graduating cohort; a cell that depends entirely on students about to graduate should trigger a succession conversation before it lapses into a forced retirement.

## Amendments

This constitution is versioned. Amendments are proposed as GitHub issues labelled `vote-required` against this repository, open for a 14-day comment period, then put to a Founding-Cell vote per `governance/voting-rules.md`. During the founding period, amendment requires unanimous agreement of both founders.

A ratified amendment is appended to `constitution/amendments/` with its date, a summary of the change, and the vote record, and `v0.md` is updated in the same pull request to incorporate the change (small, additive edits) or superseded by a `v1.md` for a structural rewrite. The amendment log is the source of truth for what changed and when; this file always reflects the current ratified state.

Semantic versioning applies at the document level: `v0` is the founding draft (pending ratification), `v1` would be the first structural rewrite, and dot-releases are not used — amendments live in the changelog, not in a fractional version number.

## Open Questions

The following are deliberately left undecided in v0. Each requires a founder decision, tracked as a follow-up issue, before the gap causes an operational problem:

1. **Legal entity form.** JSF has no defined legal structure yet (nonprofit trust, Section 8 company, unregistered association). `legal/entity-status.md` is a stub. This has downstream consequences for fundraising, liability, and IP holding.
2. **Funding model.** No policy yet exists for how grants or sponsorships, if received, would be allocated between the foundation core and individual project cells.
3. **IP assignment default.** `governance/LICENSING.md` recommends Apache-2.0 with DCO sign-off but marks the license choice itself as founder-TODO (tracked as ADR-002 in `governance/DECISIONS.md`). Whether JSF or the contributor retains copyright under that license needs explicit founder confirmation, not inference from the recommendation.
4. **Committer contribution thresholds.** The Contributor → Committer numeric bar (PR count, active-months) is undefined foundation-wide; each cell may set its own in v0, which risks inconsistency across cells.
5. **End of the founding period.** `governance/voting-rules.md` names this transition but leaves its trigger as TODO (e.g., "three or more non-founder committers active for 6+ months" vs. "formal board constituted"). Until this is set, the founding period has no defined end.
6. **Cell-spawning rights beyond the Founding Cell.** Currently only the Founding Cell can spawn a new project cell. Whether a threshold number of committers can file a charter and spawn independently is unresolved.
7. **Cross-cell guild formalization.** Guilds are described as an informal knowledge-transfer mechanism in v0; whether they need charters, leads, or voting rights of their own is undecided.
8. **JSF-level dissolution.** This document defines how a project cell retires; it does not define how JSF itself would wind down if that were ever necessary.
9. **Founder legal name.** See the note under Founders above — `roster/founders.yml` and this document use "Krishna Chaudhari"; confirm against the founder's legal identification before any external legal filing relies on this document.
10. **Provenance of the CRISPR model's external framing.** The model is adapted directly from the IIIT Nagpur CRISPR precedent (verified); an additional framing referenced during project scoping as "the CRISPR Model from Prapalaya Pinarco" has no locatable public source (see `constitution/CRISPR-model-notes.md`, §5 and Open Questions). If a primary source exists, it should be added there and cited; until then, this constitution cites only the verified IIIT Nagpur precedent and its named public analogues.
